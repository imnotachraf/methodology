II. Research Methods

The Positive Effects of AI on English Department Students' Writing Skills

This study investigates the effects of AI-assisted writing tools (ChatGPT, Grammarly, Wordtune) on the academic writing performance of undergraduate English majors.

A Mixed-Methods Approach

Systematically integrating quantitative survey data with in-depth qualitative interview findings to identify broad trends and interpret lived experiences.

1. Participants

Target: Undergraduate English Studies Students

Sampling followed a two-phase strategy to ensure both breadth and depth of representation.

Phase 1: Quantitative

  • • Method: Convenience sampling
  • • Target: 100–200 respondents
  • • Purpose: Sufficient for meaningful statistical analysis of AI tool usage patterns across the student population.

Phase 2: Qualitative

  • • Method: Purposive sampling (from survey pool)
  • • Target: 8–12 students
  • • Selection Criteria: Prioritized diversity in AI tool familiarity (from novices to frequent adopters) to capture the widest range of experiences.

2. Research Instruments

Capturing measurable patterns and contextual reasoning

2.1 Online Survey

Web-based survey distributed via Google Forms. Combines MCQs and 5-point Likert Scales.

Four Thematic Sections:

  • A Demographics & Tool Usage Frequency
  • B Perceived Impact on Writing Quality
  • C Academic Productivity & Engagement
  • D Perceived Challenges & Ethical Concerns

2.2 Semi-Structured Interviews

Open-ended format encouraging elaboration on topics unanticipated by the survey.

Three Core Themes:

Theme 1: Students' emotional investment in writing and authorial identity.
Theme 2: Specific strategies for integrating AI during drafting and revision.
Theme 3: Maintaining an authentic voice amid increasing AI use.

3. Data Collection Procedures

A two-track sequential timeline

1

Phase 1: Quantitative Survey

4 Weeks Anonymous

Accessible via a unique URL distributed on multiple social media platforms (Facebook, WhatsApp). Left open to maximize participation and accommodate different student schedules.

2

Phase 2: Qualitative Interviews

20–30 Minutes Audio Recorded

Survey respondents willing to participate were contacted individually. Sessions conducted face-to-face or via secure video conferencing (Zoom/Teams). Explicit informed consent obtained for verbatim transcription.

4. Data Analysis

Triangulating numbers and narratives

Analysis followed a two-track strategy aligned with the mixed-methods design, with findings from both tracks triangulated at the interpretation stage.

4.1 Quantitative Analysis

Survey data exported from Google Forms was subjected to descriptive statistical analysis.

  • ✓ Frequency counts
  • ✓ Percentage distributions
  • ✓ Mean scores
Identified macro-level trends in usage rates and aggregate perceptions of impact on quality/productivity.

4.2 Qualitative Analysis

Audio recordings were transcribed verbatim and subjected to inductive thematic analysis.

  • ✓ Line-level data coding
  • ✓ Grouping codes into categories
  • ✓ Synthesizing overarching themes
Emergent themes (over-reliance, ethical ambiguity) contextualized the quantitative patterns.

Methodological Rationale

Why this approach? What are we doing here?

"How are AI writing tools affecting the way English undergraduates write, and why?"
A survey tells us how many use it; interviews tell us how it feels. Together, they produce findings that are simultaneously broad and deep.

Mixed Methods

Combines numbers and narratives. Quantitative data establishes patterns; qualitative data explains them.

Survey (n=100–200)

Casts a wide net. Likert scales measure attitudes consistently. Large sample makes statistical trends meaningful.

Purposive Interviews

Targets varying AI experience deliberately. Maximizes diversity of perspectives where depth matters more than representativeness.

Descriptive Statistics

Summarizes survey efficiently. Answers: 'How often?' and 'Do most feel it improves writing?'

Thematic Analysis

Identifies patterns without forcing predefined categories. Allows unexpected findings (identity concerns) to emerge naturally.

Sequential Design

Survey establishes the landscape first. Interviews recruit from that pool, ensuring participants are contextualized within the broader sample.
